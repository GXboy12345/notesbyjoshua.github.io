# AP Biology

AP Biology studies life through a few big ideas: evolution, energy, information, and interactions. The course moves from molecules and cells to genetics, natural selection, and ecology, but the AP exam usually asks you to connect those ideas with data, experiments, and evidence.

A strong way to study is to keep asking four questions:

1. What biological system is being described?
2. What structure, process, or interaction explains the observation?
3. What evidence from the graph, table, experiment, or model supports the claim?
4. How would changing one variable affect the system?

AP Biology free-response questions often reward clear claims, careful use of experimental evidence, and explanations that connect mechanisms to outcomes. Vocabulary matters, but it works best when paired with cause-and-effect reasoning.

## Units

- **Unit 1: Chemistry of Life** - water, macromolecules, enzymes, and the chemical basis of biological structure.
- **Unit 2: Cell Structure and Function** - organelles, membranes, transport, compartmentalization, and cell size.
- **Unit 3: Cellular Energetics** - enzyme activity, photosynthesis, cellular respiration, and energy transfer.
- **Unit 4: Cell Communication and Cell Cycle** - signaling pathways, feedback, mitosis, and regulation of cell division.
- **Unit 5: Heredity** - meiosis, inheritance patterns, genetic variation, and environmental effects on phenotype.
- **Unit 6: Gene Expression and Regulation** - DNA, RNA, transcription, translation, mutations, regulation, and biotechnology.
- **Unit 7: Natural Selection** - evolution, population genetics, evidence for common ancestry, phylogeny, and speciation.
- **Unit 8: Ecology** - population dynamics, community interactions, energy flow, biodiversity, and ecosystem disruption.

## Practice

### MCQ

1. A student observes that an enzyme-catalyzed reaction slows down after the temperature is raised far above the enzyme's optimal temperature. Which explanation is most likely?

   (A) The reaction has reached equilibrium.<br>
   (B) The enzyme's active site has changed shape.<br>
   (C) The substrate concentration has increased.<br>
   (D) The enzyme has been converted into product.

2. In a population, a mutation increases the chance that an organism survives and reproduces in a specific environment. Over many generations, what is most likely to happen?

   (A) The mutation becomes more common in the population.<br>
   (B) All individuals immediately develop the mutation.<br>
   (C) The environment changes to match the mutation.<br>
   (D) Genetic variation disappears from the population.

### FRQ

3. A student is studying the effect of light intensity on the rate of photosynthesis in an aquatic plant.

   $$(A)$$ Identify the independent variable and the dependent variable in the experiment.

   $$(B)$$ Describe one control variable that should be kept constant.

   $$(C)$$ Predict how increasing light intensity would affect the rate of photosynthesis at low light levels, and justify your prediction.

## Solutions

### MCQ

<div class="theorem-box" markdown="1">

### Solution 1

At very high temperatures, many enzymes denature. Denaturation changes the enzyme's shape, including the active site, so the substrate no longer binds as effectively.

$$
\boxed{\text{B}}
$$

</div>

<div class="theorem-box" markdown="1">

### Solution 2

Natural selection can increase the frequency of a heritable trait when that trait improves survival or reproduction in a particular environment.

$$
\boxed{\text{A}}
$$

</div>

### FRQ

<div class="theorem-box" markdown="1">

### Solution 1

$$(A)$$ The independent variable is light intensity. The dependent variable is the rate of photosynthesis.

$$(B)$$ One control variable is the species or amount of aquatic plant used in each trial. Other acceptable controls include temperature, carbon dioxide availability, time interval, or water volume.

$$(C)$$ At low light levels, increasing light intensity should increase the rate of photosynthesis because light provides the energy needed for the light-dependent reactions. More available light can increase the production of ATP and NADPH until another factor becomes limiting.

</div>
